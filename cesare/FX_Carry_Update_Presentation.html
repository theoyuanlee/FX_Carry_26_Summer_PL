Corporate Treasury / Global Funding · progress since the baseline review

Bottom line up front

The 2007–2026 carry premium is an EM phenomenon and a compensated risk premium — and it is remarkably hard to beat. A simple vol-targeted, inverse-vol EM+G10 book earns a net Sharpe of 0.47 (5.2%/yr). Every enhancement we tested since the baseline — smarter weighting schemes, dynamic hedges, momentum, regime timing, and two novel signals (option-implied crash skew, cross-currency funding basis) — fails to beat that simple book net of costs. The value-add is disciplined risk management, not signal complexity. That "nothing beats the simple book" result — standard and novel — is the headline for the final report.

2Return drivers — what the risk premium is

Three complementary regressions, all Newey–West, tell one coherent story: this is a compensated risk premium, not unexplained alpha.

(a) It is a levered, risk-managed carry factor — not a new anomaly

Regressed on the academic FX factors (Dollar + high-minus-low carry, "HML"), the combined track loads 1.39× on HML (t = 42.5), R² = 0.69 — it is essentially a levered version of the known carry factor. Its alpha over those factors is 2.1%/yr but not statistically significant (t = 1.6). The honest framing: we do not claim alpha versus the known carry factor. Our edge is construction — costs, sizing, tail control — and the EM-vs-G10 insight.

(b) The premium is compensation for crash / funding-stress risk

Add crash proxies and the track loads significantly negative on EMBI sovereign-spread widening and on crash-signed risk-reversals: it loses precisely when credit and funding stress spike. That is why the return is left-skewed (−0.65) — you are paid to hold the tail. This is the textbook "carry = compensated crash risk" result, confirmed in our data.

(c) The economic license — the forward-premium (UIP) puzzle holds in-sample

The pooled Fama regression of next-month spot changes on the forward premium gives b = 0.73 (t = 4.5, n = 6,713 currency-months) versus the theoretical prediction of b = 1. High-carry currencies do not depreciate enough to erase their rate advantage — that gap is the carry premium. This is the economic reason the strategy earns anything at all.

▸ Talking points
  • "The strategy earns a well-understood risk premium — a levered carry factor whose returns are compensation for crash and funding-stress risk, licensed by the UIP puzzle. It is not unexplained alpha."
  • If asked "is there alpha?" — not versus the known carry factor (2.1%/yr, t 1.6). The edge is risk management, net-of-cost discipline, and the EM-vs-G10 call.
  • One honest caveat: these strategy-level macro betas exist today per-currency; rolling them into a single strategy-level exhibit is a quick next-step build for the report.

3When it works — and when it fails

A transparent percentile-composite regime classifier (VIX + FX implied vol + EMBI) shows exactly where the premium is earned.

The carry premium is a calm-market phenomenon. It earns a Sharpe near 0.6 in Low-stress and ~0.9 in Moderate regimes — but essentially nothing in Crisis, at roughly 1.5× the volatility. The ~6% of days flagged Crisis isolate exactly the known episodes: 2008 GFC, 2015–16 China/EM, 2020 COVID, 2022 risk-off. This is the flip side of the crash-risk finding above — and it is why we tested (and rejected, net of costs) every de-risking overlay.

4Portfolio construction — which weighting is best, and why

Held everything fixed except within-leg weighting, and re-vol-targeted each scheme to 10% so the comparison is scale-free — the differentiators become net-of-cost efficiency and tail shape.

Inverse-volatility weighting (size down the volatile names) is the best net-of-cost scheme. Mean-variance optimization is the worst: with μ = noisy monthly carry it optimizes on estimation error — it churns (highest turnover), concentrates into the position cap, and inherits a far fatter left tail (max drawdown −52% vs −29%, skew −1.15 vs −0.65). Equal-risk-contribution is a near-tie that only re-weights for correlation, so its edge dies after costs. No scheme has significant alpha over inverse-vol (all |t| < 2).

5Phase 3 — the search for a novel edge

Beyond "long EM carry," using data most desks don't have: full FX option surfaces and the cross-currency funding basis. Two directions done — both honest nulls, which is itself a defensible, publishable result.

D1 — Crash-risk-premium / option-implied skew carry NULL

Thesis: the risk-reversal prices how expensively each currency's crash is already insured — two currencies with equal carry but different skew are not the same trade. We built a full battery (carry cleaned of skew, the skewness-risk-premium of Li–Sarno–Zinna, directional tilts). Outcome: no variant beats the matched carry book. The most interesting result is a reversal of the published claim: rather than the skewness-risk-premium subsuming carry, on our data carry keeps a significant alpha over the skew premium (t = +2.2), while the skew premium earns none over carry (t = −0.4).

D3 — Cross-currency basis / dollar-funding carry NULL — the Global-Funding-relevant one

Thesis: post-2008, covered interest parity fails; the basis measures the dollar-funding premium (Du–Tepper–Verdelhan) — literally your desk's language. We tested it as both a funding-stress conditioner and a cross-sectional signal. Outcome — null, but instructively so: the basis is only measurable where we have onshore fixings, which confines the tradable universe to 7 restricted EM names with no G10. On that narrow universe carry itself is negative (a Turkish-lira crisis artifact), so nothing clears the bar. The funding-stress index does light up correctly at Lehman-2008 and the 2020 COVID dollar squeeze — it measures what the literature says — but de-risking on it barely moves the book. Critically, this null is data-limited, not conceptual — and that sets up the top data ask.

▸ Talking points
  • "Two independent novel signals now join the four standard enhancements in failing to beat the simple book. The premium is robust and hard to improve on — that is the honest, and I'd argue valuable, headline for the report."
  • If asked "is the funding-basis idea dead?" — no, it is data-limited. With direct G10 basis swaps and a SOFR funding leg it becomes the real Du–Tepper–Verdelhan test. That is the top data ask.

6What's next

1 · New data (the hunt)

A prioritized shopping list is compiled. Highest value: for D3, a USD SOFR-OIS funding leg (the window is capped at Sept-2024 by USD LIBOR's discontinuation, not by our EM data) plus direct G10 cross-currency basis swaps — which would give us the G10 dollar-funding basis the literature actually studies. For D2: an investable FX vol-carry benchmark and higher-frequency spot for cleaner realized vol. Free win: we already own unused 10-delta option wings and 5-tenor vol surfaces.

2 · D2 — FX volatility risk premium

The last novel signal: harvest implied minus realized volatility — a premium distinct from directional carry. Can start now on data already in hand.
